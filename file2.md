# Structured Study Guide: Entrepreneurship - Writing a Business Plan

## Topic 1: Introduction to Business Plans
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Business Plan**: A written narrative, typically 25 to 35 pages long, that describes what a new business plans to accomplish.
*   **Dual-Use Document**: A document used both inside and outside the firm.

**Core Concepts & Relationships**
*   **The Two Primary Audiences**: 
    *   **A Firm’s Employees**: They are looking for a clearly written plan that helps them operate in sync and move forward in a consistent and purposeful manner.
    *   **Investors and Other External Stakeholders**: They are looking for a case to be made that the firm is a good use of an investor’s funds or the attention of others.
*   *Relationship*: The business plan serves as a bridge aligning internal operations (employees) with external validation and funding (investors).

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The dual-use nature of a business plan and the differing motives of the two primary audiences. 
*   *Why*: Understanding who reads the plan and what they are looking for is foundational to understanding why a business plan is written in a specific way.

---

## Topic 2: Guidelines for Writing a Business Plan
**Core Concepts**
*   **Structure**: A business plan should follow a conventional structure to make the best impression. Departing from the basic structure is usually a mistake because investors are busy people who want to easily find critical information.
*   **Tone**: The plan needs to project a sense of anticipation and excitement about the possibilities surrounding the new venture, along with facts and figures.
*   **Content Length**: The plan must be long enough to provide sufficient, clear, and concise information on all important aspects, yet short enough to maintain reader interest. For most plans, 25 to 35 pages is sufficient.
*   **Evolution of the Plan**: It is important to recognize that the written plan will usually change, as new insights invariably emerge when entrepreneurs immerse themselves in writing and receive feedback.

**Examples Mentioned**
*   **Software Packages (Jira)**: Interactive, menu-driven software packages like Jira exist to assist in writing a plan. However, entrepreneurs should avoid boilerplate plans that look like they came from a "canned" source.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The recommended length (25-35 pages) and the reasoning against using highly creative or unconventional structures.
*   *Why*: Instructors want to ensure students know how to format documents to meet standard investor expectations.

---

## Topic 3: Types of Business Plans (Framework)
**The Three-Tier Business Plan Framework**
This model categorizes business plans based on their length, target audience, and the venture's stage of development:
1.  **Summary Business Plan**: 
    *   *Length*: 10–15 pages.
    *   *Purpose*: Works best for new ventures in the early stages of development that want to "test the waters" to see if investors are interested in their idea.
2.  **Full Business Plan**: 
    *   *Length*: 25–35 pages.
    *   *Purpose*: Works best for new ventures that are at the point where they need funding or financing. It serves as a "blueprint" for the company's operations.
3.  **Operational Business Plan**: 
    *   *Length*: 40–100 pages.
    *   *Purpose*: Meant primarily for an internal audience. It works best as a tool for creating a blueprint for a new venture's operations and providing guidance to operational managers.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: Matching the type of business plan to its appropriate page length, audience, and purpose.
*   *Why*: This framework distinctly categorizes the practical applications of business plans in real-world scenarios.

## Topic 4: Outline of a Business Plan
**Full List of Steps/Sections**
While each entrepreneur must decide which elements to include, a complete suggested outline consists of:
1.  Executive Summary
2.  Company Description
3.  Industry Analysis
4.  Market Analysis
5.  Marketing Plan
6.  Management Team and Company Structure
7.  Operation Plan
8.  Product (or Service) Design and Development Plan
9.  Financial Projections

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The sequential order of the sections. 
*   *Why*: The logical flow of a business plan is critical for investor readability.

---

## Topic 5: Section 1 - Executive Summary
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Executive Summary**: A short overview of the entire business plan that provides a busy reader with everything that needs to be known about the new venture’s distinctive nature.

**Core Concepts & Rules**
*   It should not exceed two single-space pages.
*   *Key Insight*: It is arguably the most important section of a business plan. In many instances, an investor will only ask for a copy of the executive summary and will only request the entire plan if the summary is sufficiently convincing.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The maximum length (two single-spaced pages) and its role as the gatekeeper document for investors.
*   *Why*: Students must understand that without a strong executive summary, the rest of the plan may never be read.

---

## Topic 6: Section 2 - Company Description
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Company Description**: The main body of the business plan that begins with a general description of the company.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   Company description
*   Company history
*   Mission statement
*   Products and services
*   Current status
*   Legal status and ownership
*   Key partnerships (if any)

**Core Concepts**
*   *Key Insight*: While it may seem less important at first glance, it is extremely important because it demonstrates to the reader that you know how to translate an idea into a business.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The specific components that make up this section (e.g., mission statement, legal status).
*   *Why*: It establishes the foundational legitimacy and legal realities of the startup.

---

## Topic 7: Section 3 - Industry Analysis
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Industry Analysis**: A section describing the industry that the business will enter in terms of its size, growth rate, and sales projections.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   Industry size, growth rate, and sales projections
*   Industry structure
*   Nature of participants
*   Key success factors
*   Industry trends
*   Long-term prospects

**Core Concepts & Relationships**
*   *Key Insight*: A business must grasp its industry's promising areas and points of vulnerability before selecting a target market. 
*   *Relationship*: The industry largely defines the playing field that a firm will participate in, making this step a mandatory prerequisite before conducting the localized Market Analysis.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The difference between industry analysis and market analysis.
*   *Why*: Students frequently confuse the broader industry overview with the specific target market segment.

---

## Topic 8: Section 4 - Market Analysis
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Market Analysis**: The process of breaking the industry into segments and zeroing in on the specific segment (or target market) to which the firm will try to appeal.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   Market segmentation and target market selection
*   Buyer behavior
*   Competitor analysis

**Core Concepts & Relationships**
*   *Key Insight*: Most startups do not service their entire industry; instead, they focus on a specific target market within the industry. 
*   *Relationship*: Knowing consumer behavior allows the startup to tailor its products or services appropriately to that specific target market.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The focus on target markets rather than whole industries, and the inclusion of competitor analysis in this section.
*   *Why*: Demonstrates an understanding of realistic market sizing for startups.

---

## Topic 9: Section 5 - Marketing Plan
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Marketing Plan**: A section focusing on how the business will market and sell its product or service.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   Overall marketing strategy
*   Product, price, promotions, and place

**Core Concepts & Relationships**
*   *Key Insight (Steps)*: The best way to describe the marketing plan is to sequentially: 1) articulate marketing strategy, positioning, and points of differentiation, and then 2) talk about how these are supported by price, promotional mix, and distribution strategy.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The 'Four Ps' (Product, price, promotions, and place) as the core components of this section.
*   *Why*: These are fundamental marketing principles applied to business planning.

---

## Topic 10: Section 6 - Management Team and Company Structure
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Management Team**: Typically consists of the founder or founders and a handful of key management personnel.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   Management team
*   Board of directors
*   Board of advisers
*   Company structure

**Core Concepts & Relationships**
*   *Key Insight*: This is a critical section of the plan. 
*   *Relationship with Executive Summary*: Many investors and readers will read the executive summary first and then go directly to the management team section to assess the strength of the people starting the firm.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The high priority investors place on this section immediately after reading the executive summary.
*   *Why*: Highlights the real-world investor heuristic that "ideas are cheap, execution is key" (which relies on a strong team).

---

## Topic 11: Section 7 - Operations Plan
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Operations Plan**: Outlines how your business will be run and how your product or service will be produced.

**Frameworks/Models Explained**
*   **Back Stage / Front Stage Model**: A useful way to illustrate how a business runs is by describing activities in two categories:
    *   *"Back stage"*: Activities unseen to the customer.
    *   *"Front stage"*: Activities seen by the customer.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   General approach to operations
*   Business location
*   Facilities and equipment

**Core Concepts**
*   *Key Insight*: This section should be kept short and crisp to strike a careful balance between adequately describing the topic and providing too much detail.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The "back stage" vs. "front stage" conceptual model and the mandate to keep the section brief.
*   *Why*: Operational details can easily bloat a business plan; knowing how to categorize and condense them is a key skill.

---

## Topic 12: Section 8 - Product (or Service) Design and Development Plan
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Product (or Service) Design and Development Plan**: A section needed if you are developing a completely new product or service, focusing on the status of your development efforts.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   Development status and tasks
*   Challenges and risks
*   Intellectual property

**Core Concepts**
*   *Key Insight*: Many seemingly promising startups never get off the ground because product development stalls or is harder than expected. It is important to convince the reader that this will not happen to your venture.

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: Why this section exists (to mitigate development risks) and the inclusion of intellectual property.
*   *Why*: Acknowledging risks and protecting assets are major concerns for investors.

---

## Topic 13: Section 9 - Financial Projections
**Key Terms & Definitions**
*   **Financial Projections**: The final section presenting a firm’s pro forma (or projected) financial projections.

**Full List of Items to Include**
*   Sources and uses of funds statement
*   Assumptions sheet
*   Pro forma income statements
*   Pro forma balance sheets
*   Pro forma cash flows
*   Ratio analysis

**Core Concepts & Relationships**
*   *Relationship*: Financial projections come last because they take all the plans developed in the earlier sections and express them in financial terms. 

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The specific pro forma documents required and the rationale for placing this section at the very end of the business plan.
*   *Why*: Emphasizes that finances are the quantitative translation of the qualitative strategies discussed earlier.

---

## Topic 14: Presenting the Business Plan to Investors
**Core Concepts & Rules for Oral Presentations**
*   **Follow Directions**: If given 15 minutes, do not talk for more than the allotted time.
*   **Delivery**: The presentation must be smooth and well-rehearsed. The entrepreneur should also look sharp.
*   **Visuals**: Slides should be sharp and not cluttered.
*   **Preparation**: A smart entrepreneur anticipates the questions that will be asked and is prepared for investor queries.

**Full List: Twelve PowerPoint Slides to Include in an Investor Presentation**
1.  Title Slide
2.  Problem
3.  Solution
4.  Opportunity and target market
5.  Technology
6.  Competition
7.  Marketing and sales
8.  Management team
9.  Financial projections
10. Current status
11. Financing sought
12. Summary

**Exam Focus**
*   *What's likely to be tested*: The specific list of the 12 slides required for an investor presentation, and the fundamental rules of presentation (timing, rehearsal, slide clarity).
*   *Why*: Pitching is a core entrepreneurial skill, and standardizing the pitch deck format is industry standard practice.
