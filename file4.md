# Chapter 9: Building a New-Venture Team

## Introduction to New Venture Teams & Liabilities of Newness

### Key Concepts and Definitions
*   **New Venture Team:** Is the group of founders, key employees, and advisers that move a new venture from an idea to a fully functioning firm. 
*   **Liabilities of Newness:** Refers to the fact that new companies often falter because the people involved can’t adjust fast enough to their new roles and because the firm lacks a track record of success.

### Framework: Elements of a New Venture Team
The new venture team does not typically come together all at once; instead, it is built progressively as the firm can afford to hire additional personnel. The team involves more than just paid employees. The separate elements surrounding the founder or founders of a venture include:
*   Key employees
*   Management team
*   Board of directors
*   Board of advisers
*   Lenders and investors
*   Other professionals

### Concept Relationships
*   **Overcoming Failure:** New ventures have a high tendency to fail due to the liabilities of newness. Assembling a talented and experienced management team is one specific path that firms can take to overcome these limitations and adjust to new roles faster.

### Exam Focus
*   **Most likely to be tested:** The definition of "Liabilities of Newness" and how to overcome it.
*   **Why:** It is a foundational concept explaining why startups fail and directly justifies the need for building a strong, diverse new-venture team, which is the core thesis of the chapter.

## The Founder or Founders

### Key Concepts and Details
The characteristics of the founder or founders, along with their early decisions, have a significant impact on how the new venture team takes shape. 
*   **Size of the Founding Team:** Studies show that 50% to 70% of all new ventures are started by more than one individual. It is widely believed that new ventures started by a team have an advantage over those started by a single individual.

### List: Factors That Contribute to a Founder's Success
1.  **Firm Started by a Team:** Start-ups started by a team provide greater resources, a broader diversity of viewpoints, and a broader array of other positive attributes compared to individual-led ventures.
2.  **Higher Education:** Entrepreneurial skills are enhanced through higher education.
3.  **Prior Entrepreneurial Experience:** Founders who are familiar with the entrepreneurial process are more likely to avoid costly mistakes compared to founders without this experience.
4.  **Relevant Industry Experience:** Founders with this experience are more likely to have better-established professional networks and more applicable marketing and management skills.
5.  **The Ability to "Network" Effectively:** Having broad social and professional networks gives founders potential access to additional know-how, capital, and customer referrals.

### Exam Focus
*   **Most likely to be tested:** The five factors contributing to a founder's success, specifically contrasting solo founders vs. team founders. 
*   **Why:** Professors often test the empirical benefits of team-founded start-ups (resources, viewpoints) and the specific benefits of prior industry/entrepreneurial experience (avoiding mistakes, better networks).

## The Roles of the Board of Directors

### Key Concepts and Definitions
*   **Board of Directors:** A panel of individuals who are elected by a corporation’s shareholders to oversee the management of the firm. It is legally required if a new venture organizes as a corporation.
*   **Inside Director:** A person who is also an officer of the firm.
*   **Outside Director:** Someone who is not employed by the firm.

### List: Formal Responsibilities of the Board
A board of directors has three formal responsibilities:
1.  Appoint the officers of the firm.
2.  Declare dividends.
3.  Oversee the affairs of the corporation.

### Meeting Frequency and Compensation
*   Most boards of directors meet three to four times a year.
*   New ventures are more likely to pay board members in company stock or ask them to serve on a voluntary basis rather than paying a cash honorarium.

### Framework: What a Board of Directors Can Do to Help a Start-Up
Beyond formal responsibilities, the board provides critical strategic functions:
*   **Provide Guidance:** This is considered the board's most useful role, offering guidance and support to the firm’s managers.
*   **Lend Legitimacy:** Well-known and respected board members bring instant credibility to a firm, a concept also known as signaling.

### Exam Focus
*   **Most likely to be tested:** The distinction between an inside vs. outside director, the three formal responsibilities of the board, and the concept of lending legitimacy/signaling.
*   **Why:** The legal and structural definition of corporate boards is a staple of business exams. The dual purpose of the board (formal legal oversight vs. strategic guidance/credibility) is a crucial analytical concept.

## Board of Advisors

### Key Concepts and Definitions
*   **Board of Advisors:** A panel of experts who are asked by a firm’s managers to provide counsel and advice on an ongoing basis. 

### Concept Relationships: Board of Directors vs. Board of Advisors
*   Unlike a board of directors, an advisory board possesses **no legal responsibility** for the firm and gives strictly **nonbinding advice**.
*   Many people are more willing to serve on a board of advisors because it requires less time and carries no potential legal liability.
*   Similar to the board of directors, the board of advisors provides guidance and lends credibility to the firm.

### List: Guidelines for Organizing a Board of Advisors
1.  Ensure advisors play a meaningful role in the firm's development and growth, otherwise they will become disappointed.
2.  Look for board members who are compatible and complement one another in terms of experience and expertise.
3.  Carefully spell out the rules regarding access to confidential information when inviting individuals to serve.

### Exam Focus
*   **Most likely to be tested:** The exact differences between a Board of Directors and a Board of Advisors.
*   **Why:** This is a classic compare-and-contrast exam question. Students must know that advisors have no legal liability and offer nonbinding advice, making them easier to recruit for early-stage startups.

## Lenders, Investors, and Other Professionals

### Key Concepts and Definitions
*   **Lenders and Investors:** Because they have a vested financial interest in the companies they finance, they often become very involved in helping the firms fund. They assume a natural role in providing financial oversight and lending advice.
*   **Other Professionals:** This group includes attorneys, accountants, and business consultants.
*   **Business Consultant:** An individual who gives professional or expert advice. They fall into two categories: paid consultants, and consultants available for free or at a reduced rate through a nonprofit governmental agency.

### List: Ways Lenders and Investors Add Value to an Entrepreneurial Firm
1.  Help identify and recruit key management personnel.
2.  Provide insight into the markets that the new venture plans to enter.
3.  Help the venture fine-tune its business model.
4.  Serve as a sounding board for new ideas.
5.  Provide introductions to additional sources of capital.
6.  Serve on the new venture’s board of directors or board of advisors.
7.  Recruit customers.
8.  Help to arrange business partnerships.
9.  Provide a sense of stability and calm.

### Exam Focus
*   **Most likely to be tested:** The value-added roles of lenders and investors beyond just providing money.
*   **Why:** It highlights the concept of "smart money" in entrepreneurship—investors aren't just a bank; they act as recruiters, networkers, and strategic partners. Testing this ensures students understand the broader ecosystem of a startup's team.
